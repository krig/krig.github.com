The fall of some kind of empire

I am not an economist. That doesn't mean I can't have an opinion though. Sure, my predictions are probably going to turn out to be wrong. Since my predictions are pretty gloomy, that can't really be a bad thing, though.

So. Right now (that is, late spring of 2009), there is a global economic crisis rolling over the world. The cracks of the financial bubble started showing, and everyone got scared, and here we are. Well, it's not actually that bad yet. Not that many people have lost their jobs, and the housing prices here in sweden, despite being at ridiculously high levels, keep on rising.

Some people have even started saying that it's all over, back to the glory days.

Here is my bold prediction: The economic collapse has only begun. Clearly, no one knows where this is going. Economists try to make guesses based on the stories they tell themselves, and clearly the guesses of economists are not accurate predictors. For one thing, there is a nice Heisenberg effect in that if the economists in general are positive, that in itself will cause optimism that may or may not be realistic. So if the general opinion of the future is negative, that will affect the real outcome of things. Clearly, such a complex feedback system makes it very precarious to try to speak to future states with any degree of certainty.

Never mind that though, I'm giving it a try. For one, I don't think anyone listens to what I have to say.

Globally, here is what I predict:

  • America is irrelevant going forward. The dollar will cease to be the dominant currency world-wide.

  • The same housing crisis that has ravaged the United States will strike in Europe as well.

  • The giant ball of money that has been printed and is sitting in the banks too afraid to lend them out will eventually start finding its way onto the market, with inflation panic following in its wake.

  • Inflation.

Locally (as in sweden), my major expectation is that housing prices will plummet. Right now, I think that the turning point will be at a general price level of 55% of the peak.

For one, I think it is laughable when economists make optimistic noises about the recovery of the economy, while knowing that most of the impact of the rising unemployment has yet to strike. Sony Ericsson and Ericsson are doing exceptionally poorly, just like Volvo and Saab. Sure, IKEA seems to be doing well, but pretty much all the other major swedish industrial companies are on the brink of collapse. Also, I don't know much about the state of the baltic economies, but it doesn't seem terribly unlikely that collapses there can topple at least some of the big swedish banks. Oh, and I do predict inflation here as well.
